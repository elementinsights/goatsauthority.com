If you've found ticks on your goats, you might be worried about Lyme disease. The good news is that goats are more resistant to it than dogs or humans, and death from Lyme disease in goats is uncommon.

## Can goats die from Lyme disease?

Goats can get Lyme disease from the bite of an infected deer tick or black-legged tick carrying the bacteria Borrelia burgdorferi. In rare cases, untreated infections can spread to the joints, heart, and nervous system and become fatal.

However, most goats that contract Lyme disease survive it, especially with prompt treatment. Goats seem to handle the bacteria better than many other species, and plenty of goats are exposed to infected ticks without ever showing symptoms at all.

The real risk is when an infection goes unnoticed and untreated for weeks or months. That's when it can cause lasting joint damage, kidney problems, and in extreme cases, death. Other potentially fatal conditions like [cold weather exposure](/can-goats-die-in-cold-weather) can weaken a goat's ability to fight off infections like Lyme.

## Symptoms to watch for

The most common sign of Lyme disease in goats is a stiff, stilted walk. You might notice your goat moving like its joints are sore, especially after resting.

Lameness that shifts from one leg to another, swollen joints, fever, and decreased appetite are other classic symptoms. Some goats also become unusually lethargic and lose weight gradually over several weeks.

Diagnosing Lyme disease in goats is tricky because blood tests aren't always reliable. A goat can test positive for exposure without actually being sick, and false negatives happen too. Your vet will usually make the call based on symptoms, tick exposure history, and how the goat responds to antibiotics. Other diseases like [distemper are often confused](/can-goats-carry-distemper) with Lyme symptoms, making accurate diagnosis important.

## Treatment options

The standard treatment is a course of tetracycline antibiotics, typically given for two to four weeks. Most goats start showing improvement within the first few days of treatment, though the full course needs to be completed to knock out the infection.

In more stubborn cases, your vet might switch to a different antibiotic or extend the treatment period. Goats with joint swelling often benefit from anti-inflammatory support during treatment as well. Having [Benadryl in your barn kit](/can-benadryl-hurt-a-goat) is useful for managing secondary allergic reactions during treatment.

If you catch it early, the recovery rate is excellent. Most goats bounce back completely and don't have lasting problems. Long-term joint stiffness can occasionally linger in cases that went untreated for a while, but even that tends to improve with time.

## Preventing ticks on your goats

Prevention is simpler and cheaper than treatment. Keep your pastures mowed, especially along fence lines and the edges of wooded areas where ticks like to hang out.

Permethrin-based sprays and pour-on products labeled for livestock work well for tick control on goats. Talk to your vet about the right product and dosage for your herd, and reapply according to the label since one treatment won't last all season. The same products help manage [goat lice infestations](/can-goat-lice-go-into-a-dormant-stage) that share similar treatment protocols.

Check your goats for ticks regularly, focusing on the warm spots: behind the ears, under the chin, around the udder, and between the legs. Tick activity peaks in spring and fall when temperatures stay between 45 and 85 degrees, so ramp up your checks during those months.

## Can Lyme disease spread from goats to humans?

A goat can't directly give you Lyme disease. You can't catch it from their milk, their meat, or from handling an infected goat.

The danger is that the same ticks feeding on your goats are also happy to feed on you. If your goats are carrying ticks around the farm, those ticks can drop off and latch onto you during chores.

Wear long sleeves and tuck your pants into your boots when working around goats during tick season. Check yourself after every barn visit, especially if you've been in tall grass or brushy areas. While ticks are a concern, remember that [goat lice cannot live on humans](/can-goat-lice-live-on-humans), so those are species-specific parasites you don't need to worry about personally.

## Final Thoughts

Lyme disease is a real concern in tick-heavy areas, but it's rarely a death sentence for goats. Keep your pastures managed, use tick prevention products, and learn the symptoms so you can catch it early.

If you spot a goat walking stiff or going off its feed during tick season, call your vet and mention Lyme as a possibility. Early treatment makes all the difference.

## What is deworming and why is it important for pregnant goats?

Deworming is the process of removing parasitic worms from the digestive system of animals. Pregnant goats are particularly susceptible to worm infestations, which can lead to malnutrition, anemia, and even death.

Deworming pregnant goats help to make sure that they remain healthy and are able to produce healthy offspring. There are a variety of deworming products available, and it is important to consult with a veterinarian to choose the best product for your goats.

In addition, good hygiene practices, such as prompt removal of manure, will help to reduce the risk of worm infestations.

## How do you know if your goat needs to be dewormed?

If you're a goat owner, chances are you've had to deal with parasites at some point. These pesky critters can cause a variety of problems for your goat, including weight loss, anemia, and diarrhea.

So how do you know if your goat needs to be dewormed? One telltale sign is if they start to lose weight, even though they're eating the same amount of food.

Another sign is if their coat starts to look dull and lackluster. If you notice any of these signs, it's time to take your goat to the veterinarian for a checkup.

A veterinarian will likely recommend a course of deworming medication to get rid of the parasites and restore your goat to good health.

## What are the signs of worm infection in goats?

There are a few signs that may indicate a worm infection in goats. If you notice your goat acting lethargic or off their feed, it could be a sign of a worm.

Diarrhea, pale gums, and a rough coat are also common indicators of parasites. In severe cases, goats may even cough or wheeze as the worms migrate to the lungs.

If you suspect your goat has worms, it's important to have them checked out by a veterinarian to determine the best course of treatment.

## Which worms can infect goats and what are their symptoms?

There are many different types of worms that can infect goats, and the symptoms can vary depending on the type of worm. One of the most common types of worms is the stomach worm.

Stomach worms live in the goat's stomach and cause issues with digestion.

The most common symptom of stomach worms is weight loss, as the worms take away nutrients from the food the goat eats. Another type of common worm is the lungworm.

Lungworms live in the lungs and cause respiratory problems. The most common symptom of lungworms is a gunshot, as the lungs become inflamed and full of mucus.

If you suspect your goat has any type of worm, it's important to take them to the veterinarian so they can be properly diagnosed and treated.

## Tips for keeping your goats healthy and free of parasites

Goats are relatively easy to care for, but there are a few things you need to do to keep them healthy and free of parasites. First, provide them with plenty of fresh, clean water.

Goats are very thirsty animals and will drink a lot of water, so make sure their water dish is always full. Second, feed them good quality hay or grass.

Goats are browsers, not grazers, so they need a diet that includes hay or grass. Third, keep their shelter clean and dry.

Goats are susceptible to diseases if they live in dirty, wet conditions.

Finally, have your goats checked regularly by a goat expert. A veterinarian can check for parasites and other health problems and vaccinate your goats against diseases.

By following these tips, you can help keep your goats healthy and free of parasites.

### The FAMACHA Scoring System

If you're not familiar with FAMACHA, it's a quick test you can do yourself to check for barber pole worm infection. You pull down the goat's lower eyelid and compare the color to a chart that ranges from dark red (healthy) to pale white (severely anemic).

Scores of 1 and 2 mean the goat is fine. A score of 3 means you should keep an eye on her. Scores of 4 and 5 mean she needs deworming right away.

This method is especially useful for pregnant does because it helps you avoid unnecessary deworming. You only treat the animals that actually need it, which also slows down parasite resistance to dewormers.

You can buy a FAMACHA card online or get one from your vet. It takes less than 30 seconds per goat once you get the hang of it.

### Safe Dewormers vs. Ones to Avoid During Pregnancy

Fenbendazole (SafeGuard) is generally considered the safest dewormer for pregnant does. It has a wide margin of safety and is approved for use during all stages of pregnancy. Most goat vets will reach for this one first.

Ivermectin is another common choice that is generally safe for pregnant goats when used at the correct dosage. Overdosing ivermectin can cause problems, so weigh your doe before calculating the dose. Do not eyeball it.

Levamisole is one to avoid during pregnancy unless your vet specifically recommends it. This dewormer has a narrower safety margin and has been linked to issues in other livestock species when used on pregnant animals.

### Building a Rotational Grazing Plan to Reduce Worm Loads

The best dewormer is prevention. Rotational grazing breaks the parasite life cycle by moving goats off contaminated pasture before the worm larvae become infective.

Divide your pasture into at least three paddocks and rotate your goats every three to four weeks. Let each paddock rest for at least six weeks before grazing it again. During the rest period, sunlight and dry conditions kill a large percentage of the larvae on the grass.

Mowing rested paddocks down to about four inches helps too. Short grass exposes worm larvae to UV light, which is lethal to them. Tall, damp grass near the soil line is where most larvae survive and wait to be eaten.

### Keeping Records of Deworming Treatments

Write down every deworming treatment in a log book or spreadsheet. Include the date, the product used, the dosage given, and the goat's weight at the time of treatment.

This record is invaluable for tracking which dewormers are still effective on your farm. If a particular product stops working, your records will show the pattern long before you would notice it from observation alone.

Share your deworming records with your vet at each visit. They can use this information to recommend the most effective parasite management strategy for your specific herd.
